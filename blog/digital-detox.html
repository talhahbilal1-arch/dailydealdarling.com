How to Actually Put Your Phone Down (Without Willpower)

By Mia Reynolds · December 31, 2024 · 7 min read

Last week I checked my screen time: 4 hours and 47 minutes. Not my worst day. Not my best either. And here's the thing - I barely remembered using my phone. That's almost five hours just... gone. Scrolling. Refreshing. Checking notifications that didn't exist.

If you've ever told yourself "just five more minutes" and looked up an hour later, or picked up your phone to check the weather and ended up on Instagram for 30 minutes - you know what I mean. Our phones are literally designed to be addictive. Willpower alone doesn't work.

These three products have helped me actually disconnect - not because I'm disciplined, but because they remove the option to "just check real quick."

Why Willpower Isn't the Answer

Tip #1: Make it physically harder
You wouldn't keep cookies on your pillow if you were trying to eat healthier. Same logic: if your phone is locked away, you can't mindlessly grab it.
Tip #2: Replace, don't just remove
"Don't scroll your phone" leaves a void. "Read this book instead" fills it. You need a replacement activity, especially before bed.
Tip #3: Create phone-free zones
Bedroom. Dinner table. First hour after waking. Pick one place or time to start, and make it non-negotiable.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do phone lock boxes actually work?

Yes! The key is removing the option to "just check real quick." By eliminating the decision fatigue of constantly resisting your phone, lock boxes let you focus on what matters. You physically cannot access your phone until the timer ends.

Won't I miss important calls with a phone lock box?

Most lock boxes have sound holes so you can still hear calls and alarms - you just can't pick up the phone to scroll. If it's truly urgent, you'll hear it ring. But let's be honest: how often is a call actually that urgent?

Why use an old-fashioned alarm clock?

If your phone is your alarm, it's the first thing you grab in the morning and probably the last thing you look at before bed. A separate alarm clock removes that temptation and helps establish better sleep boundaries.

What's a good book light for nighttime reading?

Neck lights or clip-on book lights with amber/warm LEDs are best. They illuminate your book without disturbing a partner or suppressing melatonin like phone screens do. Look for adjustable brightness and rechargeable batteries.

The Bottom Line

I'm not anti-phone - I love my phone. I just don't love who I become when I use it mindlessly for hours every day. These tools help me use my phone intentionally instead of compulsively.

Start with one change: phone lock box during dinner, alarm clock to get phone out of bedroom, or neck light to make reading more appealing than scrolling. You don't have to do all three at once. But do something - because willpower alone isn't working for any of us.
