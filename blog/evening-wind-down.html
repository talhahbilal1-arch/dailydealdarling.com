How to Actually Wind Down at Night (When Your Brain Won't Stop)

By Mia Reynolds · December 31, 2024 · 7 min read

It's 11pm. I've been "going to bed" for the past hour. Instead, I'm lying in the dark mentally composing emails, remembering I forgot to sign that permission slip, and wondering if I turned off the stove (I did - I checked three times).

If your brain treats bedtime as prime time for anxious thoughts and tomorrow's to-do list, you're not alone. My mind used to be loudest right when I needed quiet. After years of 2am ceiling-staring, I've finally found a wind-down routine that actually tells my brain "we're done for today."

Creating a Wind-Down Routine That Works

Tip #1: Signal your brain with consistency
Same activities, same order, same time. Your brain starts recognizing these cues as "time to power down." It sounds basic because it is - and it works.
Tip #2: Engage your senses
Smell, sound, touch - sensory cues are powerful sleep triggers. A specific scent or sound that you ONLY use at bedtime becomes a Pavlovian signal for sleep.
Tip #3: Put thoughts somewhere else
If your brain won't stop listing things, give it somewhere to put them. Write tomorrow's to-do list before bed. Once it's on paper, your brain can let go.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does lavender actually help you sleep?

Research suggests lavender can reduce anxiety and improve sleep quality. It's not a miracle cure, but many people find it helpful as part of a consistent wind-down routine. The key is using it regularly so your brain associates the scent with sleep.

Are white noise machines worth it?

For many people, yes. They mask disruptive sounds and create a consistent sleep environment. Especially helpful if you have noisy neighbors, a snoring partner, live on a busy street, or have light-sleeping kids.

How long before bed should I start winding down?

Ideally 30-60 minutes. This gives your body time to transition from "alert" mode to "sleep" mode. But even 10-15 minutes of intentional wind-down is better than going from full activity to bed immediately.

What if I don't have time for a long bedtime routine?

Even 10 minutes helps. Light a candle, spray your pillow, turn on white noise. Small consistent signals tell your brain it's time to sleep. The routine matters more than the length.

The Bottom Line

I still have nights where my brain won't cooperate. But they're fewer now. The routine - candle, spray, white noise - has become a reliable signal that tells my body "we're done thinking for today."

It's not magic. It's just consistency. Pick one or two things that signal "bedtime" and do them every night. Your brain will start to get the message.
