How to Stop Hitting Snooze and Actually Become a Morning Person

By Mia Reynolds · December 31, 2024 · 7 min read

I used to hit snooze seven times minimum. Not an exaggeration. My phone alarm would go off, I'd dismiss it without fully waking up, and then panic 40 minutes later when I realized the kids needed to be at school in 20 minutes. Morning chaos was just... my life.

Becoming a "morning person" felt like something other people were born as, not something you could actually become. But after a few key changes (and these three products), I've transformed into someone who actually has time to drink coffee while it's hot. Revolutionary, I know.

Building a Morning Routine That Sticks

Tip #1: Wake up the same time every day
Yes, even weekends. Your body's clock doesn't know it's Saturday. Consistency is the single biggest factor in becoming a morning person.
Tip #2: Make the first action automatic
Don't make decisions in the morning - you have no willpower yet. Have your clothes laid out, your coffee ready to brew, your routine scripted.
Tip #3: Light is your secret weapon
Your brain wakes up to light. A sunrise alarm or immediately opening blinds signals your body that it's time to be awake.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I stop hitting snooze?

Put your alarm across the room so you have to get up. Use a loud old-school alarm or a sunrise alarm that wakes you gradually. The key is making it physically harder to stay in bed.

What time should I wake up?

Whatever gives you enough time to not feel rushed - for most people, that's 30-60 minutes before you need to leave. Consistency matters more than the exact time.

Do sunrise alarms actually work?

For many people, yes. The gradual light mimics natural sunrise and can make waking feel less jarring than a sudden loud alarm. They're especially helpful in winter when it's dark in the morning.

How long does it take to become a morning person?

About 2-3 weeks of consistent wake times. Your body's circadian rhythm will adjust, but you have to be consistent - including weekends, at least until the habit is solid.

The Bottom Line

You don't need to overhaul your entire life to have better mornings. A wake-up light, fast coffee, and an alarm you can't ignore create a foundation that makes everything else easier. Start there, and the rest will follow.
